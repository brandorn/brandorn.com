Insight

A web application used for in-depth monitoring of application performance throughout development lifecycles.

  • Technical research
  • User interviews
  • Paper and browser prototyping
  • Information visualization
  • Stylesheet development

Objective

Present relevant application metrics to help development and test teams respond to performance problems early on in the development process.

Research

Studying the way logging and diagnostic tools were used by application teams revealed that testers and developers couldn't get a broad sense of application health without running complex, cumbersome queries on each item.

Solution

A tool that consolidates useful application metrics, making them meaningful for people with varying levels of technical skill.

Impact

Application teams can quickly compare metrics against norms and standards, and run diagnostic tests based on clues gathered from correlations between various metrics.

Like a hawk released into the wild or a child sent off to college, once an application leaves the stages of local and sandbox development, one can’t really know how it’s going to do until it’s out in the world. Whether an application is working or not is relatively easy to tell. But how well it’s doing can be a more difficult, but equally important judgment.

I was asked to help a Platform-as-a-Service team make a tool that gives application teams and owners across the Systems department a clear sense of the runtime health of their applications: how available their applications are in test and production environments, how they respond to various levels of throughput, how quickly they perform operations, and other measurements. Early on, we knew that the tool needed to be accessible and useful for developers and testers with a high degree of technical skill and also for other folks who would check in from time to time on applications within their purview. Insight would have to be a flexible, efficient means for people to learn about their applications out in the wild.

An audit of the technologies used by various teams for gathering runtime data showed us a few things. One, that teams only checked on the health of their applications when told through the grapevine that something was going wrong, and two, that the complexity of the queries needed to gather health metrics meant that teams seldom ran them, and seldom ran them correctly. We realized that part of Insight's value would be to present a consolidated, standardized source of health metrics for teams across the enterprise. Its value would also be the presentation of pre-diagnostic information for sending teams in the right direction when discovering an issue, not unlike an oil-check indication that lights up well in advance of a choked-up engine.

I worked with data and development teams to catalog the types of applications to display in Insight, and the metrics that we would report for each. With a basic taxonomy and requirements established, I drew paper sketches to try different layouts. I at first considered a single-column list, displaying applications and related metrics in rows. Yet the variety of data that we eventually decided to show for each tile lent itself better to tiles.

Knowing that we’d need to display information for hundreds of discrete applications, Insight’s interface needed an obvious navigation structure to enable users to easily hone in on their interests. After refining the sketches, I created a few browser prototypes for experimenting with tabs, filters, labels, and other types of navigation conventions. We ended up settling on a combination of tabs, filters, and search. And after trying various table and grid layouts, we used tiles to represent high-level information about applications, from which "drawers" expanded to show more detailed metrics. As the number of metrics reported grew, however, we ended up keeping the tiles but dropping the drawers, as it were, instead using full-page modals to display detailed application metrics.

Sparklines came in handy for giving context to point-in-time numbers and averages. These, coupled with color-coding for threshold breaches, gave users an immediate but nuanced understanding of their applications' health.

To make the layout responsive, I needed to design the navigation in such a way that users could search for applications and still easily filter them. Instead of hiding and showing functionality, I tried to keep elements visible when possible, to make the switch from desktop to mobile that much more consistent.

What interested about this project its hybrid nature. In a sense, it's one part dashboard and one part development tool, and has since evolved within that shared space. It's highly technical yet broadly applicable to the variety of people who use it.

My role at the outset was to identify questions and assumptions for the project team to consider as we designed Insight. For my part, I needed to learn what types of information our users wanted, and what was insufficient about the information and tools they had. Interviews with teams across the department gave direction to the data and overall information architecture of our tool.

From there, I collaborated with my team by developing browser prototypes, writing styles, and mocking up functionality as necessary. I leaned on them for knowledge about the data, which informed the kind of visualizations I chose.
